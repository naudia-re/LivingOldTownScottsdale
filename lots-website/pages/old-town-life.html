The Story

The history that shaped these streets

Before it was the rooftop pools and gallery walks, Old Town was a $3.50-per-acre homestead, a Western-themed cattle town, and a postwar architectural laboratory. The neighborhood you walk today is layered with all of it.

1888 — 1909

The Founding

Winfield Scott homesteads 640 acres at $3.50 an acre. A newspaper typo turns "Orangedale" into "Scottsdale." The Little Red Schoolhouse goes up in 1909 and still stands today.

1910s — 1940s

The Western Era

Cattle, citrus groves, dude ranches, and the cowboy iconography that still threads through Old Town's brand today. The original Main Street takes shape.

1950s — 1960s

The Hallcraft Years

Hallcraft Homes, Ralph Haver, and Fred Woodworth define the postwar Old Town single-family experience. These are the homes today's buyers fight over and renovate with reverence.

Live in a Hallcraft? We'd love to feature your home.

1970s — 1990s

The Arts District Era

Fifth Avenue shops, the gallery scene takes off, and the rodeo culture cements as part of the local identity. The bones of today's walkable downtown form here.

2000s — 2010s

The Reinvention

Optima rises. The W Hotel arrives. Old Town becomes a nightlife destination and the entertainment district that out-of-towners come for and locals navigate around.

2020s — Now

The Now

The condo boom matures. The STR economy reshapes ownership. The new Civic Center reopens. Old Town is being rewritten in real time — we cover it weekly on the blog.

The Builders

The names behind the homes

Most of Old Town's character traces back to a handful of builders and architects. Knowing who built what is half the work of buying smart in this neighborhood.

Postwar · 1950s—60s

Hallcraft Homes

The volume builder that shaped most of Old Town's original single-family stock. Compact, well-sited, full of period details worth preserving.

Read the full story →

Architect · Mid-century

Ralph Haver

Phoenix's defining mid-century architect. Haver homes in Old Town are cult objects today — clean lines, low pitches, courtyard-forward floor plans.

Read the full story →

Builder · Mid-century

Fred Woodworth

Quiet but consequential. Woodworth's neighborhoods are some of the most intact pockets of original Old Town today — patient buyers wait years for one to come up.

Read the full story →

Builder · Late 20th c.

T.W. Lewis

The late-era builder behind some of Old Town's larger custom homes. Solid construction, generous lots, and floor plans that have aged well.

Read the full story →

Modern · 2010s—now

Optima

The high-rise that redefined what a Scottsdale condo could be. Vertical gardens, full amenity stacks, and the most-traded address in Old Town's luxury market.
